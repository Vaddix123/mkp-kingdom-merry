THE HARD QUESTIONS · INSTITUTIONAL DILIGENCE

Why hasn't a hyperscaler just done this themselves?
They buy, they don't assemble. Hyperscalers have world-class real-estate and power-procurement teams — they do not have biogas deal teams, landfill-operator relationships, or constructed-wetland permit specialists. Apple's Maiden NC site took 3 years to negotiate with Waste Management, and they only did one. MKP's moat is the deal assembly, not the technology.
What if the landfill operator breaches the fuel contract?
The 20-year offtake contract includes liquidated damages at 150% of incremental fuel cost during breach. The 579 other qualifying sites in EPA's LMOP inventory provide substantial mitigation. Backup: switch to pipeline RNG at $20-25/MMBtu — economics become less attractive but the facility still clears 1.1× DSCR with a fuel swap. Fuel-source risk is survivable.
What if Bloom Energy goes bankrupt?
Mitsubishi MEGAMIE, FuelCell Energy DFC, and Ceres Power all produce equivalent SOFC technology. Stack replacement is a commodity supply contract, not a bespoke partnership. Multi-source procurement is mandatory in final PPA — Bloom cannot single-source critical infrastructure without alternates in writing.
What's the exit strategy?
Three realistic exits: (1) Year 7-10 operator flip to infrastructure fund at 12-14× EBITDA = $1.2-1.8B enterprise value. (2) Year 5+ roll-up to IPO once 3+ sites are operational (Digital Realty comp: 15-20× EBITDA). (3) Any-time strategic acquisition by Microsoft, Meta, or Google who explicitly want BTM-structured sites.
What happens year 25+ when LFG production declines?
LFG production declines ~5%/yr after peak. Gas cleanup + SOFC hardware are fuel-agnostic — they accept pipeline RNG or blended feed without modification. Year 20-22 fuel-source transition to pipeline RNG at $25/MMBtu raises fuel OpEx by ~$120M/yr but facility still produces $40M EBITDA at current revenue assumptions. Payback window already closed by that point.
Why won't the utility challenge this legally?
Behind-the-meter generation is explicitly permitted under FERC rules. Exporting-only interconnects (the small 19-MW tie) are routine FERC approvals. No utility has successfully challenged a BTM industrial site. Meta and Microsoft have public FERC filings for BTM hyperscale sites that went through without legal challenge.
What about community / NIMBY opposition?
Industrial land adjacent to existing landfills — zero residential neighbors typical. The constructed wetland is a visual positive ("looks like a nature preserve"). Creates 60 permanent jobs at $125K avg burdened wage in typically-overlooked communities. Municipal water sales create a vocal local-government ally. Job and water benefits neutralize typical opposition.
What's the IRR to equity?
Base case: $1.75B CapEx with 46% equity ($805M) and $98M EBITDA less ~$72M debt service = $26M Y1 equity cashflow, growing to $40M+ post-amortization. 20-yr IRR to equity: 11-13% base, 15-17% upside. Meets infrastructure fund hurdle (Brookfield Infra Fund IV target: 12-14% net).
How do you reach financial close without fuel contract?
You don't. Sequence: (1) Signed LFG LOI with landfill operator (30-day process from intro to LOI). (2) Hyperscale tenant term sheet. (3) Bloom equipment reservation. (4) Infra debt term sheet. THEN financial close. Typical timeline: 12 months from first outreach to FID. Build starts month 13. First power month 30.
Why 100 MW specifically? Why not 50 MW or 200 MW?
100 MW hits the sweet spot. Below 50 MW, hyperscale tenants won't look. Above 150 MW, typical LFG site output can't fuel it without heavy RNG pipeline blending (economics weaken). 100 MW also matches Bloom's modular scale (Servers ship in 200 kW blocks = clean factor of 500 modules). Sweet spot of fuel supply + tenant demand + modular equipment.
What's the insurance and liability stack?
Standard infrastructure insurance: construction all-risk during build ($14M one-time); operational property damage + business interruption for tenant uptime ($8M/yr premium); environmental liability for gas handling ($4M/yr); D&O + general liability ($2M/yr). Total ~0.8% of CapEx/yr, already included in the $12M misc OpEx line.
What are the exact equipment lead times?
Bloom SOFC modules: 12-18 months from reservation to delivery. Industrial steam turbine (20 MW class): 18-24 months. Turboden ORC: 12-14 months. Gas cleanup: 6-9 months. Balance-of-plant: 6-12 months. Site work parallel-tracked. Critical path is SOFC + ST — both 18 months minimum, which sets the 30-month floor for first power.
What's the tax structure?
Master Limited Partnership (MLP) or LLC with partnership election. Pass-through taxation preferred. 45Q credit (CO2 destruction, $80/tonne if capture retrofit added) = potential $15-30M/yr. Investment Tax Credit 30% on SOFC portion = $75M one-time basis reduction. Bonus depreciation on equipment. Structured with tax equity partner (JPM, US Bank) for optimization.
Environmental justice concerns?
Landfill sites are already zoned industrial. Destroying methane is a net-positive local air-quality outcome vs. flaring (though both destroy methane, MKP also replaces grid-emissions electricity). Creates jobs in often-overlooked communities around landfills. ESG rating benefit quantifiable in tenant rent premium. EJ analysis typically favorable; documented in pre-permit engagement.
What if multiple MKP sites compete for hyperscale tenants?
Hyperscale demand is massively in excess of supply (Google+MSFT+AWS+Meta committed >$400B, pre-leasing 70-90% of supply 3-4 years out). Even at 5 MKP sites = 500 MW total, that's under 2% of their announced near-term demand. Supply-side constrained market; tenant competition among sites is not a concern for a decade minimum.
